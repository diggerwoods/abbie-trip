History Along the Way

Stories from the places you're driving through.

Wyoming - The Oregon Trail

You're driving across the same land that 400,000 pioneers crossed between 1840 and 1860 on the Oregon Trail. They traveled in covered wagons at about 15 miles per day. You'll cover their entire Wyoming crossing in a few hours. Many of the wagon ruts are still visible from the highway near Casper and Independence Rock, where travelers carved their names into the granite.

Wyoming - Women's Suffrage Pioneer

Wyoming was the first territory in the US to grant women the right to vote in 1869, a full 51 years before the 19th Amendment. When it applied for statehood in 1890, Congress threatened to deny it unless it revoked women's suffrage. Wyoming's legislature responded: "We will remain out of the Union one hundred years rather than come in without the women."

Idaho - The Nez Perce

The Snake River Valley you're driving through was home to the Nez Perce people for over 11,000 years. They were master horse breeders who developed the Appaloosa breed. In 1877, Chief Joseph led 800 of his people on a 1,170-mile fighting retreat toward Canada, one of the most remarkable military campaigns in American history. He surrendered just 40 miles from the border, saying "I will fight no more forever."

Idaho - Minidoka Internment Camp

Near where you're staying in Burley, the Minidoka War Relocation Center held over 9,000 Japanese Americans during WWII. Families were forced from their homes on the West Coast and imprisoned in this remote Idaho desert from 1942 to 1945. The site is now a National Historic Site, preserving the memory of this injustice.

Washington - The Cascade Tunnel

Near Deception Falls where you're stopping, the old Great Northern Railway punched through the Cascades. In 1910, two trains were swept off the tracks at Wellington (now Tye) by an avalanche, killing 96 people - the deadliest avalanche in US history. It led to the construction of the 7.8-mile Cascade Tunnel, the longest railroad tunnel in North America at the time.

Washington - Lynden's Dutch Heritage

Lynden was settled in the 1870s by Dutch immigrants who saw the flat, fertile land and were reminded of home. The town still celebrates its Dutch heritage with a working windmill, wooden shoe factory, and annual Holland Days festival. Many families in the area can trace their roots back to the original settlers from the Netherlands.

Vancouver - A City Built on First Nations Land

Vancouver sits on the unceded territories of the Musqueam, Squamish, and Tsleil-Waututh Nations, who have lived here for over 10,000 years. Stanley Park, where you might visit, was home to Coast Salish villages before the city was incorporated in 1886. The park's famous totem poles at Brockton Point tell stories of the Kwakwaka'wakw and Haida Nations.

Vancouver - Gastown's Wild Beginning

Vancouver's oldest neighborhood, Gastown, is named after "Gassy Jack" Deighton, a steamboat captain and storyteller who opened a saloon in 1867. The town that grew around it was destroyed by fire in 1886 (the Great Vancouver Fire burned the entire city in 45 minutes), but was rebuilt in stone and brick. The famous steam clock was actually built in 1977 to cover a steam vent.

British Columbia - The Canadian Pacific Railway

The road you're driving from Vancouver to Radium follows much of the route that thousands of Chinese laborers built through impossible mountain terrain in the 1880s. Over 15,000 Chinese workers helped build the Canadian Pacific Railway, with an estimated 600 dying during construction. They were paid $1/day (half what white workers earned) and given the most dangerous jobs, including handling dynamite on cliff faces.

Radium Hot Springs - The Ktunaxa People

The hot springs at Radium were sacred to the Ktunaxa (Kootenay) Nation for thousands of years before European contact. They used the mineral waters for healing and spiritual ceremonies. The springs emerge from deep within the Rockies at 47 degrees Celsius, heated by geothermal energy from 2-3 kilometers underground. Kootenay National Park was established in 1920 after the province traded the land to the federal government in exchange for a highway being built through the valley.

Montana - The Battle of the Little Bighorn

Just 60 miles south of Billings where you're staying, the Battle of the Little Bighorn took place in 1876. Lt. Col. George Custer led 210 men against a combined force of Lakota Sioux, Northern Cheyenne, and Arapaho warriors led by Sitting Bull and Crazy Horse. The entire command was wiped out in under an hour. It was the most significant Native American victory in the Indian Wars, though the US government responded with overwhelming force in the following months.

Montana - Billings and the Rimrocks

Billings sits beneath sandstone cliffs called the Rimrocks, formed 75 million years ago when Montana was covered by an inland sea. Captain William Clark (of Lewis and Clark) carved his name into Pompeys Pillar, a sandstone butte just 25 miles east of Billings, in 1806. It's the only remaining physical evidence of the Lewis and Clark Expedition along their entire route.

The US-Canada Border

You'll cross the longest undefended border in the world (5,525 miles). The boundary was established by the Treaty of 1846 at the 49th parallel. A 20-foot-wide strip of cleared forest called "the slash" runs the entire length so the border is visible from the air. It's maintained by the International Boundary Commission, who clears regrowth every few years.
